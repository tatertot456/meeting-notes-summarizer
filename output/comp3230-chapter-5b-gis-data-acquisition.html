Existing GIS Data — The Mash-Up Approach

▶

The most efficient data acquisition strategy is the mash-up approach — combine existing data from different sources rather than recreating what already exists. A mash-up is a web page or application combining data from multiple sources.

Since the early 1990s, U.S. government agencies at all levels have established websites for sharing public data. All non-classified federal data must be made publicly available (Freedom of Information Act).

Spatial Data Infrastructure (SDI)

A distributed system for acquiring, processing, distributing, using, maintaining, and preserving spatial data. The framework that makes geospatial data sharing possible across organizations.

Clearinghouse

Provides access to geospatial data and related online services for data access, visualization, and ordering. Data-centric — focused on making data available.

Geoportal

Offers multiple services: data links, news, references, community forum, and often an interactive data viewer. Service-centric. Examples: Data.gov, Geospatial Platform (US), INSPIRE (EU), GEOSS (global).

Idaho Resources

Inside Idaho (insideidaho.org) — Idaho's official geospatial data portal. Idaho Enterprise Open Data Portal (gis.Idaho.gov). Formats: Shapefile, ESRI Map Service, ESRI Layer File, KML.

U.S. Census Bureau — TIGER/Line Files:

  • Legal and statistical area boundaries (Census Block → Block Group → Census Tract → County → State)
  • Roads, railroads, streams, water bodies, power lines, pipelines
  • KML files available for cartographic boundaries
  • Free to download at census.gov

Natural Resources Conservation Service (NRCS) — Soils Data:

  • STATSGO2 — Digital General Soil Map of the US (vector, smaller scale)
  • SSURGO — Soil Survey Geographic Database (vector, larger scale, more detail)
  • gSSURGO — Raster version of SSURGO at 10m resolution (released 2014)

Earth Observing Satellite Systems:

Landsat / Sentinel

Long-running free satellite imagery programs. Landsat (NASA/USGS) — 7+ spectral bands, 30m resolution. Sentinel (ESA) — free European imagery.

Commercial Imagery

Planet Labs (PlanetScope, Tanager hyperspectral). Maxar (WorldView — very high resolution). Sub-meter imagery available commercially.

NASA Missions

GEDI (forest canopy LiDAR), ECOSTRESS (thermal), EMIT (mineral dust aerosols), OCO-3 (carbon). Specialized science products.

OpenStreetMap

Collaborative, crowd-sourced global vector dataset. Free to use. Constantly updated by volunteers. Good global road and building coverage.

Metadata

▶

Metadata is information about geospatial data. It is an integral part of any GIS dataset and should be prepared during the data production process.

Essential metadata questions:

  • What is the data? (content, coverage, data type)
  • Where did it come from? (source, producer, citation)
  • How was it created? (methods, processing steps)
  • When was it created? (date of collection, publication)
  • What coordinate system is used?
  • What is the source scale and intended use scale?
  • What are the use limitations or restrictions?

Metadata is critical for anyone reusing public data. Without metadata, it's impossible to know whether a dataset is appropriate for your purpose, what its accuracy limitations are, or how to properly cite it. The FGDC (Federal Geographic Data Committee) sets metadata standards for US federal data. Always create metadata for data you produce.

Data Conversion

▶

Data conversion translates GIS data from one format to another. Two main approaches:

Direct Translation

Uses a translator built into the GIS package to directly convert from one proprietary format to another. Example: ArcGIS MIF to Shapefile tool converts MapInfo format directly to shapefile. Fast and simple when supported.

Neutral Format

Government agencies translate data into a neutral/public exchange format (e.g., SDTS — Spatial Data Transfer Standard). The user then converts the neutral format to their GIS format. Works when direct translation isn't available. Like a universal adapter.

GPS & GNSS Systems

▶

Global Navigation Satellite Systems use four or more satellites to determine the x, y, z coordinates of a receiving station. Multiple nations operate independent GNSS constellations:

SystemOperator
GPS (Global Positioning System)USA — the original and most widely used
GLONASSRussia
GalileoEuropean Union
BeiDouChina
NavICIndia (regional)
QZSSJapan (regional)

GPS elevation is measured relative to the geoid (sea level surface accounting for gravity differences), NOT the spheroid. This matters for precise elevation work. Also critical: GPS units can collect data in multiple datums (NAD83, NAD27, WGS84) and projections (UTM, lat-lon). YOU MUST KNOW AND RECORD THE DATUM AND PROJECTION SETTINGS before collecting data — the default may not be what you want.

Digitizing — Scanning & On-Screen Details

▶

Snapping — automatic alignment to nearby features within a specified tolerance:

  • A new line's end can automatically snap to an existing arc if the gap is smaller than the snapping tolerance
  • A point (node/vertex) can snap to another point within the snapping tolerance
  • Snapping is essential for topologically correct digitizing — ensures features connect properly

Scanning challenges for automated vector conversion:

  • Tracing intersections — line width doubles or triples where lines meet
  • Broken lines — tracing must bridge gaps
  • Adjacent lines — distinguishing two nearby lines vs. one wide line
  • Separating lines from polygons
  • Skeletonization — reduces raster lines from multi-pixel width to single-pixel centerline (morphological erosion)

On-screen digitizing workflow in ArcGIS:

  • Create shapefile (in ArcCatalog/Catalog pane)
  • Load shapefile into map
  • Open for editing (Editor toolbar / Edit tab)
  • Add vector features (points, polylines, or polygons)
  • Save and close editing session

Source map quality is critical for digitizing accuracy. Errors in the source document are faithfully reproduced in the digitized output. Unstable paper maps can shrink or stretch unevenly. Always use the highest quality source material available, and record its source scale, date, and origin in the metadata of the digitized product.
